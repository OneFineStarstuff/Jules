## 1. Multi-Layered Governance Architecture

The governance of Artificial Intelligence requires a coordinated, multi-layered approach that harmonizes global principles with local enforcement and technical rigor.

### 1.1 International Bodies: Establishing Normative Primacy
International organizations such as the **United Nations (UN)** and the **OECD** play a critical role in establishing the foundational normative principles that guide AI development. Their primary mandate is the creation of global treaties (e.g., a "Treaty on Autonomous Weapon Systems") and high-level ethical guidelines that ensure AI serves as a public good. These bodies facilitate diplomatic consensus on the "Unacceptable Risks" that transcend national borders.

### 1.2 National Governments: The Enforcement Pillar
National governments are the primary engines of enforcement. They translate international norms into domestic law and manage public sector adoption.
- **The EU AI Act Model:** The European Union’s risk-based approach serves as the global blueprint for national legislation.
    - **Article 5 (Prohibited Practices):** Establishes a "Redline" against practices such as social scoring and mass biometric surveillance.
    - **Article 6 (High-Risk Systems):** Mandates rigorous conformity assessments for AI used in critical infrastructure, healthcare, and law enforcement. This provides a clear statutory framework for ensuring transparency and human oversight (Art. 14) before market entry.

### 1.3 Industry Consortia & Standards Organizations
Technical standard-setting bodies like **ISO/IEC** (specifically ISO 42001) and the **IEEE** bridge the gap between policy and engineering. They provide the certification frameworks and interoperability protocols (e.g., EAIP) that allow organizations to prove compliance with national laws through objective, measurable technical controls.

## 2. Mechanisms for Collaboration

Isolationism in AI safety research is a systemic risk. We propose two primary mechanisms for cross-border cooperation.

### 2.1 Information Sharing: The AI Incident Reporting Format (AIRF)
To move from reactive to proactive governance, we propose the **AIRF (AI Incident Reporting Format)**. This standardized JSON schema allows for the automated sharing of system vulnerabilities and "near-miss" failure events across borders.
- **Fields:** `incident_vector`, `model_architecture`, `epistemic_failure_type`, `mitigation_status`.
- **Protocol:** A secure, cryptographically anchored hub where national regulators can share anonymized telemetry without leaking sensitive corporate intellectual property.

### 2.2 Joint Research: A "CERN for AI Safety"
The complexity of AI alignment requires pooling global intellectual and compute capital. A multilateral "CERN for AI Safety" would focus on:
- **Mechanistic Interpretability:** Mapping the "Honesty Manifold" of frontier models.
- **Formal Verification:** Developing mathematical proofs of safety for non-deterministic systems.
- **Multilateral Auditing:** Providing a neutral ground for the "Safety Case" review of systems exceeding $10^{26}$ FLOPs.

## 3. Risk Mitigation Strategies

### 3.1 Tiered Risk Assessment
Governance must be proportional to capability. We propose the following criteria for tiered classification:
1. **Tier 1 (Narrow):** Low-impact task automation. Minimal oversight.
2. **Tier 2 (Sensitive):** Systems with significant data impact (PII/MNPI). Requires ISO 42001 hardening.
3. **Tier 3 (Frontier/AGI-Adjacent):** Defined by **Compute Thresholds** (>10^25 FLOPs) or **Capability Impact** (e.g., autonomous coding, bio-design). Requires mandatory multilateral audit.

### 3.2 Compute Governance: KYC for Cloud
Compute is the single most verifiable input in the AI lifecycle.
- **Mechanism:** Implement "Know Your Customer" (KYC) requirements for high-performance cloud providers.
- **Trigger:** Cloud vendors MUST report any customer utilizing >10,000 H100-equivalents to national regulators to ensure that unaligned "Wildcat" training runs are technically impossible.

### 3.3 Red Teaming & The "Safety Case" Requirement
Frontier model developers MUST submit a comprehensive **Safety Case** before deployment.
- **Content:** Detailed evidence of adversarial robustness (Red Teaming), bias mitigation results, and the presence of an **Inherent Risk Mitigation Interface (IRMI)** hardware kill-switch.
- **Audit:** Safety Cases are reviewed by independent third-party auditors and anchored to a public "Provenance Ledger" for non-repudiation.
